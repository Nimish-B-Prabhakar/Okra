## Executive Summary

June 2026 marks the founding month of Okra as both a product and a research platform. This report documents the full arc of work completed: from the initial product hypothesis through market research, problem decomposition, prompt engineering experiments, research harness construction, and the first empirical findings from live LLM testing.

The month produced seven documented research findings, a validated six-criterion judge rubric, two confirmed research questions with four sub-questions each, a live testing harness with database logging and analytics, and the first evidence that the core research hypothesis — LLMs can extract personality signal from sparse, curated dating profile text and use it to generate engaging social interactions — is both testable and partially supported, with important documented failure modes.

---

## 1. The Starting Point — Why This Project Exists

### 1.1 Market context

The US dating app market generated approximately $1.45 billion in revenue in 2026, but the industry recorded its first annual revenue decline in 2025, falling 1.7% globally to $6 billion — the first decline since Tinder launched in 2012. The dominant platforms are visibly contracting: Tinder's paying users fell from 9.6 million to 8.8 million over 2025, Bumble's stock collapsed 91% from its all-time high, and Bumble's paying users fell 8.7% in a single quarter.

Against this, niche apps that solve a specific problem — Hinge (up 25% in 2025), Grindr (up 26%), PURE (up 46%) — are growing. The market is not dying. It is fragmenting toward products that actually serve their users.

The most important data point from the market research: **80% of US dating app users report an inability to find genuine connection as their primary frustration.** This is not a matching algorithm problem. It is a product architecture problem.

### 1.2 The core failure modes

Five layers of failure were identified and documented through literature review and market data analysis:

**Layer 1 — Platform design:** The swipe mechanic trains the brain to evaluate humans the way it evaluates products. Physical attractiveness is the single most predictive variable in swiping behavior — more than bio, job, or stated values (Witmer et al., 2025, N=44,435). Text-only communication strips out 93% of human signal. The hyperpersonal effect means users build inflated impressions through text that collapse on first meeting.

**Layer 2 — The psychology of choice:** The paradox of choice research (Schwartz; Iyengar and Lepper, 2000) shows that more options reduce satisfaction with any choice. Dating apps offer thousands of potential matches. Users with high FOMO initiated more relationships but those relationships lasted three times shorter with 40% lower satisfaction. The infinite supply paradox makes commitment feel like settling.

**Layer 3 — Attachment psychology:** In a study of 44,435 dating app users, 44% had an anxious attachment style and only 37% were securely attached. Apps provide intermittent validation — the most addictive reward schedule in behavioral psychology — locking anxious users in a loop without ever resolving their need. Avoidant users use apps for controlled, low-stakes engagement that allows them to disengage the moment real closeness appears.

**Layer 4 — Profile distortion:** Cornell University research found 81% of online daters misrepresent at least one attribute. The hyperpersonal effect means users build idealized impressions that don't survive a first meeting. Profiles capture attributes but cannot capture personality.

**Layer 5 — Social and cultural erosion:** A 2025 UK cohort study found dating app use was specifically associated with greater loneliness, while general social media showed no such effect. The apps that were supposed to solve connection are making loneliness worse.

## 2. The Product Response — Okra's Design Philosophy

### 2.1 The core principle

Okra is designed around a single premise: an app whose explicit success metric is that users leave because they found someone. This is structurally at war with every business model in the current industry, where the most successful users — the ones who find partners and delete the app — represent the worst possible retention outcome.

Every product decision flows from this premise.

### 2.2 Problem 1 — The swipe mechanic

**Solution designed:** A two-stage matching system that demotes photos structurally rather than removing them entirely.

Stage 1 (Discovery): Profile card shows two personality prompts and a mandatory audio clip. No clear photos — only a blurred background presence confirming a real person exists. Audio must complete before a comment box appears. A minimum 15-word comment on one prompt is required before the photo reveal button activates. This creates a behavioral commitment ladder: each step increases psychological investment before the next gate opens.

Stage 2 (Mutual match): Both users simultaneously receive the other's remaining prompts plus two photos (one face, one context), framed as "completing the profile" rather than a photo judgment. Both confirm before conversation opens.

**Key research finding that informed this:** The reverse halo effect — perceived physical attractiveness increases when positive emotional context about a person already exists. By the time photos appear, the viewer has read, listened to, and written about the person. The photo lands on top of an impression rather than creating one from scratch.

**Why full photo removal was rejected:** The reveal cliff-edge problem. Full photo removal shifts the reveal to after emotional investment has been made through texting, and Stanford Human-AI Interaction Lab data showed 74% of matched pairs felt disoriented when appearance didn't match expectations at reveal, even when they liked the person. The graduated reveal approach — present but structurally demoted — is the right balance.

### 2.3 Problem 2 — Swipe fatigue and conversation volume

**Solution designed:** A 7-conversation cap. Maximum seven active conversations at any time. To add a new match, you must delete one existing conversation. This forces real decisions about who you're actually interested in rather than accumulating matches as a form of validation.

Combined with a phased progression system: text phase (3 days), mandatory audio call, audio message phase, date request system where one person proposes times and the other confirms. Ghosting is eliminated not by social pressure but by structural design — the system moves conversations forward or closes them, it doesn't let them linger indefinitely.

### 2.4 Problem 3 — Texting as the only medium

**Solution designed:** The game system. A series of asynchronous interactive experiences that can be played independently — one person plays their turn, submits, and the other receives a notification and plays theirs. Results reveal when both have played.

The core insight driving the game design: **games create shared experience, texting creates information exchange.** Chemistry builds on shared experience and spontaneous reaction, not on learning someone's job and hometown. The right games produce moments — small revelations, unexpected agreement, funny disagreements — that texting at its best only rarely produces.

## 3. Profile Architecture — What to Show and When

### 3.1 The three-tier information reveal

All profile information is revealed progressively, tied to the matching phases:

**Tier 1 (Discovery):** Two personality-bearing prompts, audio clip. No photos.

**Tier 2 (Expanded view, pre-photo):** Emotional availability signal, one concrete value, basic logistics chips (children preference, relationship structure, distance).

**Tier 3 (Post-mutual match):** Vulnerability/self-knowledge prompt, lifestyle prompt, one explicit dealbreaker, two photos.

### 3.2 The structured fields

Six fields were designated as dealbreakers — things where a mismatch means the relationship cannot work long-term regardless of chemistry: dating intention, children preference, relationship structure, sexuality, smoking status, drinking status.

Five fields were designated as preferences — significant but navigable, varying by person, shown separately from dealbreakers with different visual weight: political values, religion, gender identity/pronouns, cannabis, lifestyle pace.

**Research backing:** 71% of singles say intentions should be discussed in first few interactions (Kinsey Institute, 2025). Children compatibility is cited as the clearest dealbreaker in OkCupid's matching system. Smoking is the most-answered question in OkCupid's entire system across all demographics. Political alignment is a dealbreaker for 40% of left-leaning daters and only 17% of right-leaning daters — enough to include as optional, not enough to make mandatory.

**Fields explicitly excluded:** Height (low relationship success predictor despite heavy initial weighting), income (women rank it 8th of 10 partner factors), education level (predicts economic sorting not compatibility), astrological sign (flat p-values across 68,371-user OkCupid dataset), body type descriptors (reintroduces appearance filtering through self-description).

### 3.3 The prompt engineering

Profile prompts were redesigned as conviction-extraction tools rather than generic conversation starters. The distinction matters for the research: a prompt that produces "I love hiking, cooking, and travel" gives the LLM nothing to work with. A prompt that produces "Quit a job I was good at because it was making me someone I didn't like. Took three weeks to tell anyone." gives the LLM real material.

A minimum 15-word answer requirement was implemented at the product level — not as a guideline but as a hard gate. "Typical Sunday... naps" is gone. A prompt cannot be submitted until it clears the threshold.

The prompt questions themselves were engineered to be impossible to answer generically. "The most uncomfortable decision I made recently was" cannot be answered with a list of interests. "What actually brought me here is" cannot be answered with "looking for someone genuine." The question's own framing prevents the most common form of profile curation.

## 4. The Game System — Design and First Experiments

### 4.1 Why games over conversation

Two people playing a game together learn more about each other in three minutes than thirty minutes of texting — not because games are entertaining as a feature, but because the right game mechanics force spontaneous, unscripted self-revelation. Text conversation is optimized for curation; games create moments that are harder to optimize.

The research framing: which game mechanics produce the behavioral signals that indicate genuine emotional investment? The hypothesis, grounded in Premack and Woodruff's theory of mind research, is that **prediction-based mechanics** — where you have to model the other person's mental state, not just share your own — produce stronger connection signals than preference-revelation mechanics. Getting a prediction right feels like being seen. Getting one wrong opens a specific, productive conversation.

### 4.2 The full game taxonomy

Ten games were designed across four mechanic categories:

**Prediction category:** Prediction Round, Concert Builder (prediction element) — user must model the other person's mental state.

**Preference revelation:** This or That, One Has to Go, The Rank — user discloses own preferences.

**Judgment/Opinion:** The Situation, Unpopular Opinion — user makes a judgment or states a view.

**Collaborative creation:** Build It Together, The Story, Two Truths and a Lie — users create something jointly.

**Design constraint for all games:** Completable in under 3 minutes. Produces a reveal moment both people react to simultaneously. Low enough stakes to play early in a conversation. Creates a natural, specific conversation moment after — not "what did you think?" but something generated from the actual result.

**Async-first design:** Every game is designed for independent play. One person plays, saves, and submits. The other gets a notification and plays independently. Neither person needs to be online at the same time. Results reveal only when both have submitted.

### 4.3 Critical design observation — fun vs. clinical

Early game conceptualization produced situations that felt like personality assessments: "A close friend sends you a draft of their passion project but it misses the mark. Do you point out the flaws directly or highlight what works first?"

The breakthrough came from reframing the prompt instruction away from "reveal personality dimensions" toward "write like a witty friend at 2am." The resulting questions — "You accidentally make intense, unbroken eye contact with a stranger while eating a massive, structurally compromised burrito. Sauce is everywhere. A: Double down, wink, and take another massive bite. B: Drop the burrito and pretend to get a text." — produce a completely different user experience. Someone reads situation 3 and immediately wants to know what their match would pick.

The mechanism: clinical questions describe the situation neutrally and ask for a behavior. Fun questions put you in a specific, slightly absurd moment that makes you smile before you even pick. The reveal happens through the laugh, not through the options being labeled as "empathetic vs. direct."

**This was documented as Finding 1** in the formal research record: prompt framing has a larger effect on output quality than input data richness.

## 5. The Research Component — LLM Game Generation

### 5.1 The formal research questions

Two research questions were formalized with IRB-appropriate pre-specification of hypotheses:

**RQ1:** Can LLMs extract meaningful personality signal from sparse, curated dating profile text, and use it to generate contextually relevant social interactions that produce higher user engagement than non-personalized alternatives?

**RQ2:** Which structural features of lightweight, asynchronous game mechanics produce measurable signals of increased emotional investment in early-stage digital relationships?

Full sub-questions and hypotheses are documented in `okra_research_formalization.md`.

### 5.2 The two-pipeline architecture

**Pipeline 1 — Prompt-mined forced binary:** Scans profile prompt answers for conviction-bearing statements (things said with intensity, emotional weight, or qualifying detail that signals genuine investment) and forces two of them into an impossible binary choice. The Jenn example: "Would you rather never watch Philly sports again, or have a kid?" — mining "watching Philadelphia sports, although it's not always simple" against "the D to my INK (dual income no kids)."

**Pipeline 2 — Keyword-selection games:** Uses structured category selections (values, music, hobbies) to generate games. Same mining principle as Pipeline 1: find internal tensions within a person's own selections before generating cross-person comparisons.

### 5.3 The sufficiency check gate

Before generation is attempted, a lighter-weight call checks whether the profile has enough conviction-bearing material to support Pipeline 1. A profile that reads "I like hiking, traveling, and good food" fails this gate. A profile that reads "although it's not always simple" in reference to something it cares about passes.

This gate prevents wasted generation calls on profiles that will produce generic output regardless of prompt quality. It also produces its own research data: tracking gate accuracy against manual judgment of output quality is one of the primary analysis tracks in Phase A of the research.

### 5.4 The judge system

Every generated question is scored against a six-criterion binary rubric by a second LLM call:

1. References real profile content (not invented scenario)
2. Genuine forced binary (no "it depends," no implied correct answer)
3. Both sides cost something the person said they value
4. Under 20 words
5. Avoids clinical language
6. **Comparable stakes** (added June 2026, see Section 6)

Binary criteria throughout — not 1-5 scales — based on the LLM-as-judge literature finding that binary rubric items produce more consistent, reproducible scores than open scales, which drift between calls.

## 6. Research Findings — June 2026

### Finding 1 — Prompt framing matters more than input richness

**Date discovered:** Mid-June 2026, manual testing phase.
**Evidence:** A sparse five-keyword input ("Spontaneous, Compassionate, Witty, Caring, Grateful" vs "Spontaneous, Straightforward, Sarcastic, Fitness, Open-minded") with a creatively framed prompt ("write like a witty friend at 2am, not a therapist") produced significantly more engaging game content than a rich four-paragraph-answer input with a clinically framed prompt ("reveal personality dimensions"). The difference was qualitative and obvious: the first version produced "You accidentally make intense, unbroken eye contact with a stranger while eating a massive, structurally compromised burrito"; the second produced "A close friend sends you a draft of their passion project but it misses the mark."
**Implication:** For the game generation use case, prompt design is the primary quality lever, not input data volume. This has direct implications for the product architecture — a smaller, well-designed set of conviction-bearing profile fields is more valuable than a comprehensive battery of questions that produces more but lower-quality signal.

### Finding 2 — Confidence calibration is poor and directionally wrong

**Date discovered:** First dry run with Gemini 1.5 Flash, June 2026.
**Evidence:** The model assigned 1.0 confidence to inference from four curated paragraph answers and 0.75 to inference from five keyword selections — backwards from what calibrated uncertainty should reflect given relative data richness. A well-calibrated model should express lower confidence on richer data that happens to be more curated, because the curation itself is a source of noise.
**Implication:** Maps directly to RQ1c. LLMs treat curated dating profile text with the same confidence as authentic text. They cannot distinguish between "this person is genuinely this way" and "this person has presented themselves this way." This is a documented capability gap with implications for any personalization system built on self-reported data.

### Finding 3 — Mining beats inventing

**Date discovered:** Jenn profile test, late June 2026.
**Evidence:** The highest-quality questions came from extracting conviction-bearing statements already present in profile text and forcing them into conflict — not from generating original fictional scenarios. "Would you rather never watch Philly sports again, or have a kid?" was not invented; it was extracted from "watching Philadelphia sports, although it's not always simple" and "the D to my INK (dual income no kids)." The question could only have been written for Jenn, and it took two seconds for a human to intuitively generate once the technique was understood.
**Implication:** The LLM task for Pipeline 1 is fundamentally extraction and conflict-construction, not creative invention. This is a more constrained task and LLMs perform it more reliably. The prompt should instruct mining, not storytelling.

### Finding 4 — Most profile content is not usable for Pipeline 1

**Date discovered:** First harness test batch, June 2026.
**Evidence:** The sufficiency check correctly failed generic answers across multiple test profiles. "Typical Sunday... naps" and "I like hiking, traveling, and good food" have no conviction-bearing material and cannot support forced-binary generation regardless of prompt engineering quality. The 15-word minimum at the product level increases the probability of usable content but does not guarantee it.
**Implication:** A real pipeline needs both the product-level minimum word count and the algorithm-level sufficiency check. The two work together: the word count increases the raw material available, the sufficiency check decides whether that raw material is actually usable. Some profiles will always fall through to Pipeline 2 regardless.

### Finding 5 — Models don't know when to stop, but can be instructed to

**Date discovered:** First harness test batch, June 2026.
**Evidence:** Early prompt versions asked for a fixed count (e.g., "generate 4 questions") from profiles with insufficient distinct material. The model manufactured near-duplicate questions rather than reporting that only 1-2 genuinely distinct questions were supportable. The Jenn test produced three variations of the same Eagles/kids tension framed differently.
**Later resolution:** After adding explicit variable-count permission and a required `skipped_reason` field, a later test correctly stopped at one question and provided detailed reasoning: "Profile A only produced one genuinely loaded pairing. Profile B's answers are each stated with real weight but don't create a funny or painful collision with each other."
**Implication:** LLMs will pad output to hit a stated target count unless explicitly instructed not to. Variable-count instructions with required self-reporting produce more honest, higher-quality output than fixed-count instructions.

### Finding 6 — The five-criterion judge rubric has a structural blind spot: comparable stakes

**Date discovered:** First harness test batch and adversarial testing, June 2026.
**Evidence:** The original five-criterion judge gave 5/5 to "Lose the joy of searching for tide pool creatures or disavow housing as a human right?" — a question that scored 1/5 manually and was immediately identifiable as broken. To confirm this was systematic rather than a borderline case, two synthetic adversarial profiles were constructed specifically to bait this failure at maximum severity:

Profile A prompts: "My go-to coffee order is an oat milk cortado, and I will judge you if you put sugar in it. Non-negotiable." and "I believe the earth is round and basic, settled scientific facts shouldn't be up for debate."

Generated question: "Would you rather believe the earth is flat, or put sugar in every oat milk cortado?"

Judge score: 5/5. Human score: 1/5.

The gap is structural: "both sides cost something" is satisfied by almost any pairing, because almost anything costs something in a literal sense. The rubric had no mechanism for checking whether the two costs are _comparable_ — whether they belong on the same scale of seriousness.

**Resolution:** A sixth criterion, `comparable_stakes`, was added with four contrastive worked examples (two pass, two fail drawn from actual test data) and an explicit default-to-false instruction for uncertain cases. Subsequent testing confirmed the criterion correctly flagged the adversarial questions while leaving good questions unaffected. The judge now scores out of 6, not 5.

**Research significance:** This is evidence that LLM-as-judge systems built from surface-structure criteria can miss failure modes that are immediately obvious to human readers. The rubric checked the skeleton of a question correctly while being blind to whether the joke could actually land. This is documented as the primary contribution to RQ1d: where LLM-as-judge scoring diverges from human quality assessment and why.

### Finding 7 — Generator self-awareness precedes judge flagging

**Date discovered:** Post-rubric-fix testing, late June 2026.
**Evidence:** In a run using the updated six-criterion rubric, the LLM's own `why_this_works` rationale for a flagged question explicitly described the comparable-stakes problem before the judge ran: "Her deepest moral conviction directly threatens the small joy she just said makes her whole morning." The generator articulated the exact failure mode that the judge then caught.
**Implication:** The model may have implicit awareness of the comparable-stakes problem that the original rubric never gave it a channel to surface. This raises a research question worth pursuing: does generator self-awareness of stakes mismatch correlate with judge `comparable_stakes` accuracy? If so, it may be possible to use the generator's own rationale as a pre-filter rather than a separate judge call — reducing cost and latency.

## 7. The Testing Harness — Technical Progress

### 7.1 What was built

A standalone internal research tool, separate from the main Okra app codebase, built on the same stack (FastAPI, React/TypeScript via Vite, PostgreSQL with asyncpg). Features implemented in June:

- Profile input UI with manual entry, screenshot OCR via vision-capable LLM call, and saved profile library
- Sufficiency check endpoint with logging (pass/fail, confidence, reason, conviction statements found)
- Generation endpoint with full logging (model used, latency, token counts, estimated cost, raw response)
- Judge endpoint with six-criterion binary rubric scoring
- Inline manual scoring interface (1-5 with notes, saved on click)
- Judge vs. manual comparison display with per-criterion breakdown
- Analytics dashboard with summary metrics (total runs, average scores, agreement rates)
- CSV export for offline statistical analysis
- Database schema supporting full research arc: from prompt iteration through behavioral ground truth once games enter the live app

### 7.2 Database schema

Seven tables implemented: `profiles`, `profile_prompts`, `profile_keywords`, `prompt_versions`, `generation_runs`, `generated_questions`, `manual_scores`, `judge_scores`. Future table `behavioral_outcomes` designed and included in schema, unpopulated until games enter production.

Key design decisions: prompt versions are never overwritten (new row per change, ensuring historical queries remain accurate), all costs are logged per call, judge scores are stored per criterion (not just total), raw API responses stored for debugging.

### 7.3 Model migration

Testing began with Gemini 1.5 Flash via REST API. During June, the harness was migrated to Claude Sonnet via the Anthropic SDK as the primary model for both generation and judging. Both models remain available via `model_used` field — cross-model comparison is planned as a research track (same profiles and prompts, different models, logged separately).

### 7.4 Rate limiting

Hit Gemini free tier limits (20 requests/day for gemini-2.5-flash) during active testing. This accelerated the migration to Claude. Rate limiting also surfaced a practical research design constraint: testing volume during early iteration phases needs to be planned against API tier limits, particularly for the sufficiency check which fires twice per run (once per profile).

## 8. What the Research Is Not Claiming

This section is included because intellectual honesty about the limits of current evidence is important both for the integrity of the research and for the PhD application narrative.

**Not claimed:** That the product design is validated. The product decisions described above are hypotheses, not findings. None of the product's core claims — that personality-first matching produces better connections, that the 7-conversation cap changes behavior meaningfully, that the game system produces connection — have been tested with real users. That testing begins when the product launches.

**Not claimed:** That LLM game generation is reliably good. The findings so far are about failure modes and conditions for success, not success rates at scale. Finding 3 (mining beats inventing) is a direction, not a validated system. The current pipeline produces good questions under specific conditions that are still being characterized.

**Not claimed:** That the judge is reliable. The judge rubric has been validated against a small set of test cases (n<15) using adversarial construction. That is enough to confirm the existence of the comparable-stakes failure mode and the effectiveness of the fix. It is not enough to claim general judge reliability. That comes with scale.

**Not claimed:** Deep learning or RL are involved. The ML in this project is the right tool for the right job: LLM API calls for generation and judging, logistic regression and gradient boosted trees planned for the sufficiency classifier once sufficient labeled data exists, k-means clustering for user behavioral archetypes. The methodology is honest about this distinction and the reasons for it.
