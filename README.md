# Okra — Personality-First Dating App + LLM Research Platform

> *Can a large language model read between the lines of a dating profile and generate something genuinely human?*

---

## What This Is

Okra is two things built on top of each other.

The first is a dating app built around a single, uncomfortable observation: current dating apps are financially incentivized to keep you single. Their algorithms optimize for session time, not successful relationships. The swipe mechanic trains people to evaluate humans the way they evaluate products. And text-based conversation — the primary medium of every major app — strips out most of the signals that actually predict chemistry: voice, spontaneous reaction, humor timing, the way someone moves through an awkward moment.

Okra is an attempt to fix the structural problem rather than the surface one. It replaces swipe-first matching with a personality-first flow, limits active conversations to seven at a time, and introduces a system of lightweight interactive games designed to replace texting as the primary mode of getting to know someone. The core thesis is that two people doing something together — even something small and slightly absurd — build more genuine connection than two people exchanging information in a chat window.

The second thing Okra is: a research platform for a specific, underexplored question at the intersection of NLP and human-computer interaction.

---

## The Research Question

**Can large language models extract meaningful personality signal from sparse, curated dating profile text — and use that signal to generate personalized social interactions that produce measurable engagement?**

Dating profiles represent an extreme edge case in NLP: the input text is short, strategically curated, and designed to present an idealized rather than authentic self. A person writing "I quit a job I was good at because it was making me someone I didn't like" is giving you real signal. A person writing "typical Sunday... naps" is not. Can an LLM tell the difference? And if it can, what does that actually mean for what it can infer?

This question has two parts that are studied in parallel:

**Part 1 — LLM capability under sparse, curated conditions.** Most existing work on LLM personality inference uses rich text corpora: social media histories, long-form writing, extended conversations. Dating profiles are a different beast. Entries are short, single-context, and performatively constructed. The research tests whether LLMs can extract actionable personality signal from this class of text, and documents the specific failure modes when they can't.

**Part 2 — What interaction mechanics produce connection.** Once matched, what kinds of structured game interactions actually accelerate genuine connection between strangers? The app tests this by comparing game mechanic categories — prediction-based games (where you model the other person's mind), preference-revelation games (where you just share your own), collaborative creation — against behavioral outcomes: post-game conversation rate, conversation depth, progression toward a real date.

This is embedded PhD research under Professor Edoardo Airoldi's program at Temple Fox School of Business, starting Fall 2026.

## The Game System — Core Innovation

The central product hypothesis is that two people playing a game together learn more about each other in three minutes than they would in thirty minutes of texting. Not because games are "fun" as a feature — because the right game mechanics force a kind of spontaneous, unscripted self-revelation that text conversation rarely does.

The specific mechanic developed and tested in this project is the **forced-binary situational question**, generated by an LLM from the actual content of two matched users' profiles.

The approach: rather than inventing generic scenarios and hoping personality shows through in the reactions, the system mines each profile for conviction-bearing statements — things said with real emotional weight, specificity, or identity stake — and forces them into an impossible binary choice. The humor and tension come from real material colliding, not from an invented setup.

Example of the mechanic working:

A profile reads: *"watching Philadelphia sports, although it's not always simple"* and *"the D to my INK (dual income no kids)."* The generated question: **"Would you rather never watch Philly sports again, or have a kid?"**

That question could only have been written for that person. It makes them laugh and then makes them think. And it tells their match something real about how they hold their identity — which is exactly what 40 minutes of "what do you do for fun" would never surface.

## Research Architecture

### Pipeline 1 — Prompt-Mined Forced Binary

**Input:** Free-text profile prompt answers (minimum 15 words each, enforced at the product level).

**Process:** LLM scans for conviction-bearing statements — things stated with intensity, emotional weight, or qualifying detail — then forces two of them into a binary with no escape route. Variable output count: 1-4 questions depending on how much material the profile actually contains, never padded.

**Gate:** A sufficiency check runs before generation to determine whether a profile has enough real material. Generic answers ("naps," "I like hiking, traveling, and good food") fail the gate and don't trigger generation — the system knows when it doesn't have enough to work with.

### Pipeline 2 — Keyword Selection Games

**Input:** Structured multi-select fields (values, music taste, hobbies, lifestyle signals) chosen from curated lists during onboarding.

**Process:** LLM identifies intersection and contrast across keyword selections, generates This or That, One Has to Go, and Concert Builder type content. Same mining principle: find the internal tensions within a person's selections before generating cross-person comparisons.

### The Judge System

Every generated question is evaluated by a second LLM call against a six-criterion binary rubric before it can be used:

| Criterion | What it checks |
|---|---|
| References real content | Question pulls from specific profile statements, not invented scenario |
| Genuine forced binary | No "it depends," no implied correct answer, no escape route |
| Both sides cost something | Either choice requires giving up something the person said they value |
| Under word limit | 20 words maximum — punchy, not clinical |
| Avoids clinical language | No therapy or HR-adjacent phrasing anywhere |
| Comparable stakes | Both options sit at a similar level of seriousness — trivial vs. trivial, or identity-level vs. identity-level, never a moral conviction against a coffee order |

The sixth criterion was added after empirical testing showed the first five passed questions that were structurally correct but obviously broken to any human reader — a settled scientific fact paired against a lifestyle preference, for example. The rubric catches the skeleton of a good question; comparable stakes catches whether the joke actually lands.

## What's Been Found So Far

This section is updated as research progresses.

### Finding 1 — Prompt framing matters more than input richness
A sparse five-keyword input with a creatively framed prompt produced more engaging game content than a rich paragraph-answer input with a clinically framed prompt. The model's output quality is bounded primarily by how the task is framed, not by how much data it receives.

### Finding 2 — Confidence calibration is poor and directionally wrong
Early tests showed the LLM assigning 1.0 confidence to inference from rich data and 0.75 to inference from sparse keyword data — backwards from what calibrated uncertainty should look like. The model doesn't distinguish between "these answers are clear" and "these answers are performatively curated." This is a documented capability gap that connects directly to the core research question about LLM inference in curated-text environments.

### Finding 3 — Mining beats inventing
The highest-quality outputs came from extracting genuine conviction-bearing statements already present in profile text and forcing them into conflict — not from generating original fictional scenarios and hoping personality showed through. The Philadelphia sports vs. having a kid question was not invented; it was extracted from what was already there.

### Finding 4 — Most profile content is not usable
Generic prompt answers contain no conviction-bearing material and cannot support Pipeline 1 generation regardless of prompt engineering quality. This motivated both the 15-word minimum at the product level and the sufficiency-check gate in the pipeline.

### Finding 5 — Models don't know when to stop (but can be taught to)
When asked for a fixed number of outputs from a profile with insufficient distinct material, early prompt versions manufactured near-duplicate questions to hit the count. The current prompt explicitly permits variable output with required self-reporting on why fewer questions were generated. Later tests showed the model correctly reasoning through why it stopped at one, citing exactly the mechanism that was failing in earlier versions.

### Finding 6 — The judge rubric fails on comparable stakes without explicit instruction
The original five-criterion judge gave 5/5 to "Would you rather believe the earth is flat, or put sugar in every oat milk cortado?" — a maximum-severity incommensurability test constructed specifically to bait the failure. This confirmed the gap was structural, not a borderline case. The sixth criterion was added with four contrastive worked examples and an explicit default-to-false instruction, and subsequent tests correctly flagged the failure while leaving genuinely good questions unaffected.

### Finding 7 — Generator self-awareness precedes judge flagging
In one notable run, the LLM's own `why_this_works` rationale for a generated question explicitly described the stakes mismatch that the judge then flagged — suggesting the model has implicit awareness of the comparable-stakes problem that the original rubric never gave it a channel to surface. This raises an interesting question for future work: does generator self-awareness of stakes mismatch correlate with judge accuracy, and can it be used as a cheaper signal than a separate judge call?

## Research Questions (Formal)

**RQ1:** Can large language models extract meaningful personality signal from sparse, curated dating profile text, and can that signal be used to generate contextually relevant social interactions that produce higher user engagement than non-personalized alternatives?

- *RQ1a:* What features of profile text are most predictive of LLM-generated game relevance as rated by users?
- *RQ1b:* Does LLM-generated personalization improve game completion rates relative to templated control games?
- *RQ1c:* What categories of personality inference does the LLM perform well vs. poorly on, given the sparse and curated nature of profile text?
- *RQ1d:* Does LLM-as-judge scoring of generated game content correlate with real user engagement outcomes, and where does it diverge?

**RQ2:** Which structural features of lightweight, asynchronous game mechanics produce measurable signals of increased emotional investment in early-stage digital relationships?

- *RQ2a:* Do prediction-based game mechanics produce stronger post-game conversation signals than preference-revelation mechanics?
- *RQ2b:* What is the relationship between game completion and subsequent conversation depth and phase progression?
- *RQ2c:* Are differential engagement effects moderated by user demographics?

## Technical Stack

**App:** FastAPI backend, React/TypeScript frontend via Vite 8, PostgreSQL with raw SQL/asyncpg, Anthropic SDK, Anthropic Claude for game generation and judging.

**Research harness:** Standalone internal tool built on the same stack — profile input UI, generation pipeline, judge pipeline, manual scoring interface, analytics dashboard, CSV export for offline statistical analysis.

**Models used:** Claude Sonnet (primary, generation and judging), Gemini 1.5 Flash (early testing, deprecated in active harness).

## Statistical Methodology

Designed for honest small-n research with appropriate methods:

**Judge-manual agreement rate** is reported with Wilson score confidence intervals rather than normal approximation, appropriate for small sample proportions.

**Quality comparisons across prompt versions** use the Mann-Whitney U test rather than t-tests — the 1-5 ordinal manual score is not normally distributed and the samples are small. Bootstrap confidence intervals (1000 resamples) around mean differences.

**Sufficiency check accuracy** is reported with standard classification metrics (precision, recall, F1) and exact binomial confidence intervals.

**Sample size targets:** ~200 game events per condition for primary analyses (based on 80% power at α = 0.05 for a 10-percentage-point detectable effect). Currently in pre-pilot, accumulating labeled examples.

All hypotheses were pre-specified before data collection. Confirmatory vs. exploratory analyses are clearly distinguished.

## Why This Research Matters Beyond the App

The broader implication of what's being tested here is about a gap in how LLMs are deployed in personalization contexts. Most LLM-driven personalization assumes the input text is authentic. Dating profiles are a case where it structurally cannot be — the entire point of the profile is strategic self-presentation in a context of evaluation. If LLMs perform well on this class of input, that's a useful capability claim. If they don't, that's a more important finding: it suggests LLM-based personalization in high-curation environments (job applications, dating profiles, professional bios, any context where people are optimizing how they present themselves) may be more fragile than generally assumed.

Either outcome is publishable. The research is designed to find the truth rather than confirm the hypothesis.
